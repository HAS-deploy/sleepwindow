social jet lag — sleep glossary

What it means

Social jet lag is the mismatch between your body's internal clock (circadian rhythm) and the sleep schedule you keep on workdays versus free days. Your biology may want you to sleep from midnight to 8 a.m., but a 6 a.m. alarm forces you to wake two hours early on weekdays. On weekends, you sleep in to "catch up," effectively flying across time zones and back every week — without leaving your home.

The term was coined by chronobiologist Till Roenneberg to describe this recurring weekly disruption. It's measured in hours: the difference between the midpoint of your sleep on free days versus work days. Someone sleeping 11 p.m.–7 a.m. on weekdays but 1 a.m.–9 a.m. on weekends has a social jet lag of two hours.

Why it matters

Social jet lag is relevant any time your schedule doesn't align with your natural chronotype. Night owls experience it most severely because most work and school start times are built around early-morning norms. Even a one- to two-hour weekly mismatch is associated with poorer mood, reduced concentration, increased fatigue, and difficulty maintaining a healthy weight. It also compounds sleep debt: sleeping in on weekends feels restorative but keeps pushing your internal clock later, making Monday mornings harder.

It comes up practically when people notice they feel alert and rested on Sunday mornings but groggy every weekday despite getting the same number of hours. The hours are the same; the timing isn't.

Example

A graphic designer naturally falls asleep around 1 a.m. and wakes feeling rested at 9 a.m. Her job requires her to be at her desk by 8 a.m., so she sets an alarm for 6:45. On weekends she sleeps her natural schedule. Her sleep midpoint shifts by roughly two hours between weekdays and weekends — two hours of social jet lag, repeated 52 times a year.

Common confusions

Sleep debt is about quantity — not getting enough total hours. Social jet lag is about timing — sleeping at the wrong point in your circadian cycle, even if total hours are adequate.

Circadian misalignment is the broader umbrella term covering any desynchrony between internal clock and external schedule, including shift work. Social jet lag is a specific, common pattern that affects people with conventional jobs who simply have a later chronotype than their schedule demands.

Regular jet lag involves crossing actual time zones and typically resolves in days as your body adjusts to local light cues. Social jet lag never fully resolves because the cycle resets every week, preventing any real adaptation.
