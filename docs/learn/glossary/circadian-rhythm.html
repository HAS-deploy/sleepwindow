circadian rhythm — sleep glossary

What it means

Circadian rhythm is your body's internal 24-hour clock that regulates when you feel awake and when you feel sleepy, along with dozens of other biological processes like body temperature, hormone release, and metabolism. The word comes from the Latin circa diem, meaning "around a day." This rhythm is primarily set by light exposure — your brain's suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) detects light through your eyes and uses that signal to synchronize the clock to the outside world.

The rhythm isn't perfectly passive. Even in complete darkness, your body will continue cycling on roughly a 24-hour schedule, though it drifts slightly without light cues to anchor it. This internal timing system explains why you tend to feel naturally alert and drowsy at consistent times each day, even before you check a clock.

Why it matters

Circadian rhythm comes up whenever sleep feels off-schedule. Jet lag is essentially your rhythm being misaligned with the local day-night cycle. Shift workers who sleep during the day fight their rhythm constantly, which is linked to long-term health consequences. Even something as common as staying up until 2 a.m. on weekends and waking early on Monday is a mild form of circadian disruption — sometimes called social jet lag.

Light from screens at night, irregular meal times, and inconsistent wake times can all weaken the strength of your circadian signal, making it harder to fall asleep quickly and wake feeling rested.

Example

Someone flies from New York to London, a five-hour time difference. Their circadian rhythm still expects sleep at midnight New York time, which is 5 a.m. in London. For the first few days they feel alert late at night and groggy in the morning — that mismatch between internal clock and local environment is circadian disruption in action. It typically takes one day per time zone crossed to fully re-synchronize.

Common confusions

Circadian rhythm vs. sleep pressure (homeostatic drive): These are two separate systems. Sleep pressure is the biochemical buildup of adenosine in the brain the longer you stay awake — it has nothing to do with time of day. Circadian rhythm is clock-based. You can have high sleep pressure but a circadian signal keeping you alert, which is why pulling an all-nighter feels manageable at 4 a.m. but brutal at 9 a.m.

Circadian rhythm vs. sleep cycle: A sleep cycle is the 90-minute progression through sleep stages (light, deep, REM) that repeats throughout the night. Circadian rhythm is the broader 24-hour schedule governing when sleep happens, not what happens inside it. The two interact — your circadian phase influences how much deep sleep versus REM sleep you get at different points of the night — but they are distinct concepts.
