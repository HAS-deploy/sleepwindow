ultradian rhythm — sleep glossary

What it means

An ultradian rhythm is any biological cycle that repeats more than once within a 24-hour day. The prefix ultra- means "beyond," referring to a frequency beyond (i.e., faster than) a single daily cycle. Sleep is the clearest example: throughout the night, the brain cycles through stages of light sleep, deep sleep, and REM sleep roughly every 90 minutes. That repeating 90-minute loop is an ultradian rhythm. Other examples include hormone pulses, appetite surges, and natural shifts in alertness and focus that occur during waking hours.

Ultradian rhythms sit in contrast to circadian rhythms, which complete one full cycle per day, and infradian rhythms, which take longer than a day to complete. All three are governed by biological timekeeping systems, but they operate on very different clocks and serve different functions.

Why it matters

Understanding the ultradian rhythm of sleep helps explain why sleep timing within the night matters, not just total duration. Waking up mid-cycle — in the middle of deep slow-wave sleep — leaves you feeling groggy and disoriented, a state called sleep inertia. Waking near the end of a cycle, when sleep is lighter, feels far more natural. This is why some people find that sleeping 7.5 hours (five full 90-minute cycles) feels better than sleeping 8 hours but waking mid-cycle.

During the day, the same basic rhythm continues as the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle (BRAC), a concept introduced by sleep researcher Nathaniel Kleitman. Roughly every 90 minutes, the brain shifts toward a lighter, more internally focused state — sometimes noticed as a dip in concentration, a wandering mind, or a sudden desire to stretch or snack. Recognizing this pattern can help you schedule demanding cognitive work during peak alertness windows.

Example

Someone sets an alarm for exactly 8 hours of sleep. They wake up feeling inexplicably foggy, even though they slept "enough." The likely reason: they interrupted a slow-wave sleep stage partway through their fifth cycle. If they had set the alarm 20 minutes earlier or later, they might have caught a lighter stage and woken feeling refreshed.

Common confusions

Ultradian vs. circadian: The terms sound similar, and people often use "circadian" loosely to mean any body clock process. Circadian rhythms specifically span roughly 24 hours (sleep/wake, body temperature, cortisol). Ultradian rhythms are shorter and repeat multiple times a day.

Ultradian rhythm vs. sleep stage: A sleep stage (like REM or Stage 3) is a specific phase within the cycle, not the rhythm itself. The ultradian rhythm is the full repeating pattern — the whole loop from light sleep through deep sleep and back — not any single stop along the way.
