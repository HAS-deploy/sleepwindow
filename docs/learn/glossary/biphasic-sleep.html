biphasic sleep — sleep glossary

What it means

Biphasic sleep is a sleep pattern in which a person sleeps in two distinct segments within a 24-hour period rather than one continuous block. The most common version combines a longer nighttime sleep with a shorter daytime nap. Historically, one well-documented form involved sleeping for a few hours in the early night, waking for an hour or two, then sleeping again until morning — sometimes called "first sleep" and "second sleep." The modern version more often refers to a main night sleep plus a brief afternoon nap.

Why it matters

Understanding biphasic sleep matters when you're trying to figure out whether your sleep schedule is unusual or problematic. Many people naturally wake in the middle of the night and feel anxious about it, not realizing this pattern has documented historical precedent and may simply reflect an innate human tendency. It also comes up in discussions about napping cultures — many societies build an afternoon rest into daily life, which is essentially biphasic sleep by design. If you consistently feel a strong urge to nap in the early afternoon and sleep well at night, you may be naturally biphasic rather than sleep-deprived.

Example

Someone goes to bed at 10 p.m., sleeps until 2 a.m., reads or does light activity for an hour, then sleeps again from 3 to 6:30 a.m. Total sleep is around 7.5 hours, but it arrives in two segments. Alternatively, a person sleeps 11 p.m. to 6 a.m. and takes a 20-minute nap after lunch — this is the more common modern biphasic pattern.

Common confusions

Biphasic vs. polyphasic sleep. Biphasic means two sleep segments; polyphasic means three or more. Polyphasic schedules (like the Uberman or Everyman) are far more extreme and typically involve multiple short naps replacing most nighttime sleep. Biphasic sleep, by contrast, usually still includes a substantial nighttime anchor and is much closer to conventional sleep habits.

Biphasic vs. insomnia. Waking in the middle of the night feels like insomnia, but the key difference is how you feel during the waking period. In a natural biphasic pattern, the waking window is calm and relaxed. Insomnia typically involves frustration, racing thoughts, and difficulty returning to sleep. Mistaking one for the other can lead people to seek treatment for something that isn't actually a disorder.

Biphasic vs. simply being tired. A midday nap driven by chronic sleep deprivation isn't the same as a stable biphasic rhythm. True biphasic sleepers feel rested after their main sleep period and take their nap as a scheduled, natural part of their day — not as compensation for a short night.
