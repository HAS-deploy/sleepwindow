SleepWindow vs AutoWake: for waking at the right sleep phase

SleepWindow vs AutoWake: Which App Actually Wakes You at the Right Sleep Phase?

Both apps promise to spare you from that groggy, dragged-out-of-deep-sleep feeling. But they take fundamentally different approaches to the problem, and the right choice depends almost entirely on what you value more: simplicity or active sensing.

How Each App Works

SleepWindow: Math-Based Cycle Planning

SleepWindow works backwards from your desired wake time — or forwards from your intended bedtime — using the well-established 90-minute sleep cycle model. The average person cycles through light, deep, and REM sleep roughly every 90 minutes. If you fall asleep at 11:00 p.m. and need to wake at 7:30 a.m., that's five complete cycles, which SleepWindow flags as an optimal window.

The app adds a short sleep-onset buffer (typically 14–15 minutes) to account for the time it actually takes you to fall asleep after lying down. The result is a set of suggested bedtimes or wake times calculated entirely in advance — no hardware needed, no microphone running overnight.

AutoWake: Real-Time Movement and Heart Rate Detection

AutoWake (available on Apple Watch) monitors your actual heart rate and movement data throughout the night. It uses this biometric stream to estimate which sleep phase you're in at any given moment. When your alarm window opens — a configurable range, usually 20–30 minutes before your target time — AutoWake fires the alarm as soon as it detects you entering light sleep or a natural wake transition.

This is the core distinction: SleepWindow predicts the right moment using averages, while AutoWake tries to detect it in real time.

Where SleepWindow Has the Edge

Zero hardware friction. You don't need a smartwatch, a charged device strapped to your wrist, or a subscription to a wearable ecosystem. Open the app, enter your numbers, go to bed. That's it.

Platform accessibility. SleepWindow works on Android, which AutoWake does not support at all. For non-iPhone users, AutoWake isn't even an option.

Privacy. No biometric data is collected or transmitted. SleepWindow runs its calculations locally and keeps nothing.

Consistency for shift workers or travelers. If your schedule is irregular, the math-based model adapts instantly. You just plug in a new bedtime or wake target and get fresh suggestions — no re-training period, no adaption phase.

Simplicity as a feature. Some people find sleep apps that produce detailed tracking reports anxiety-inducing. If you just want a better alarm time and nothing else, SleepWindow's restraint is genuinely appealing.

Where AutoWake Has the Edge

Individual variation is real. The 90-minute sleep cycle is a population average, not a personal fact. Some people run on 80-minute cycles, others on 100-minute ones. AutoWake is at least attempting to respond to your actual body rather than a statistical model.

Dynamic night adjustment. If you wake up at 3 a.m. and can't get back to sleep for an hour, SleepWindow's pre-calculated alarm is now misaligned. AutoWake recalibrates because it's watching your data in real time.

Rich sleep data. If you actually want to understand your sleep — trends, REM duration, restless periods — AutoWake gives you that. SleepWindow tells you nothing about what happened after you fell asleep.

Smart alarm window. Waking during a natural transition is genuinely gentler than being jolted awake mid-deep-sleep. AutoWake's sliding alarm window is a meaningful real-world benefit if you're flexible about your exact wake time. SleepWindow has no equivalent feature.

The Honest Limitations of Each

SleepWindow's limits: The 90-minute model is a starting point, not a guarantee. It assumes you fall asleep quickly, that your cycles are average length, and that nothing disrupts the pattern overnight. There's no feedback loop. If the predicted wake time lands in the middle of deep sleep, the app has no way of knowing or correcting for it.

AutoWake's limits: Wrist-based sleep tracking — even from Apple Watch — is far from perfect. Consumer wearables have meaningful error rates in sleep-stage classification. AutoWake is making intelligent guesses from noisy data, not reading a polysomnography. It also requires your Apple Watch to be charged and on your wrist all night, which some people find uncomfortable.

Pricing

SleepWindow is free or very low cost (typically a one-time purchase or free with optional tips), with no subscription required. The total lifetime cost is minimal.

AutoWake is also affordable — around $2–4 as a one-time purchase — but it implicitly requires an Apple Watch, which starts at roughly $250 new. If you already own one, the marginal cost is low. If you don't, AutoWake is an expensive suggestion.

Who Should Use SleepWindow

  • Android users (AutoWake isn't available to you)
  • Anyone who doesn't own or want to own a smartwatch
  • People who want a friction-free, no-setup alarm improvement
  • Travelers, shift workers, or anyone with irregular schedules
  • People who find sleep tracking data stressful rather than helpful
  • Those who want a privacy-preserving option with no ongoing data collection

Who Should Use AutoWake

  • Apple Watch owners who already sleep with their watch on
  • People whose schedule is consistent enough that a 20–30 minute alarm window is acceptable
  • Anyone who wants actual sleep stage data alongside their wake optimization
  • People who've tried cycle-calculation apps and found the fixed alarm still leaves them groggy
  • Those who want the system to adapt to disrupted nights automatically

The Honest Bottom Line

If you already wear an Apple Watch to bed, AutoWake is the better choice for most people. Real-time sensing — even imperfect sensing — beats a one-size-fits-all average. The flexible alarm window alone justifies using it over a fixed calculator, and the sleep data is a genuine bonus.

But if you don't have an Apple Watch, or you want something that simply works with zero setup on any phone, SleepWindow is the smarter pick. The 90-minute cycle model has decades of sleep science behind it, and for many people it works well enough to make a noticeable morning difference. It's also the honest answer for anyone who just wants a better bedtime calculation without turning their sleep into a data project.

Neither app is magic. Both are attempts to solve a real problem — the rigid alarm — with different tools. Match the tool to what you actually have and how you actually sleep.
