Jet lag: pre-shifting your sleep window

What Pre-Shifting Actually Does

Jet lag happens because your circadian rhythm — the roughly 24-hour internal clock that controls sleepiness, core body temperature, cortisol, digestion, and dozens of other processes — stays anchored to your home time zone while the world around you moves on. Flying eight hours east doesn't instantly reset anything; your body still wants to sleep at 3 p.m. local time and be alert at 2 a.m.

Pre-shifting is the practice of gradually moving your sleep window in the days before departure so that your clock is already partway adjusted when you land. Instead of arriving five hours out of sync, you might arrive one or two hours out of sync — a much easier gap to close.

It works because the circadian system can only shift by about one to two hours per day under ideal conditions. If you're flying from New York to London (five hours ahead), waiting until you land gives your body five days of rough adjustment. Start shifting three days before and you've already closed most of that gap.

Before You Start: Direction Matters Enormously

The strategy is completely different depending on which way you're flying.

Eastward travel requires phase-advancing — moving your sleep earlier. Your body finds this harder than delaying, which is why eastward jet lag tends to feel worse.

Westward travel requires phase-delaying — staying up later and waking later. This aligns with the natural tendency of the circadian clock to drift slightly past 24 hours, so most people find it more manageable.

Figure out the time difference and direction before doing anything else. A rough rule: if the destination is more than six hours away, pre-shifting is especially worthwhile. Under four hours, a lighter approach or simple light management on arrival may be enough.

Eastward Protocol: Shifting Earlier

How much to shift, and how fast

Aim to shift your sleep window by 30–60 minutes per day. Moving faster than 60 minutes per day tends to produce fragmented sleep, which defeats the purpose. If you have three days before departure, you can realistically advance your window by 90–180 minutes — meaningful progress for a five- or six-hour difference.

Example: You normally sleep 11 p.m.–7 a.m. Flying to a destination five hours ahead. Three days out, go to bed at 10:00 p.m. and set your alarm for 6:00 a.m. Two days out: 9:15 p.m. to 5:15 a.m. One day out: 8:30 p.m. to 4:30 a.m. You've shifted by about 2.5 hours. You'll land already partway adjusted.

Morning light is your main lever

Light is the dominant signal that resets the circadian clock. In the morning — specifically in the first two hours after your adjusted wake time — get as much bright light as possible. Go outside, sit near a window, or use a light therapy lamp (10,000 lux for 20–30 minutes). This pushes your clock earlier, which is exactly what you want for eastward travel.

Conversely, avoid bright light in the evening. Dim lights after 7 p.m. on pre-shift days, use warm-toned bulbs, and cut screen brightness. Bright evening light will fight your attempt to fall asleep earlier and blunt the advance you're trying to achieve.

Melatonin timing for eastward shifts

Low-dose melatonin (0.5–1 mg) taken 5–6 hours before your new target bedtime can help signal that night is coming. This is exogenous melatonin used as a timing cue, not a sleeping pill — the dose matters. Higher doses (the 5–10 mg pills common in pharmacies) are largely a marketing artifact and may actually make the timing signal noisier rather than clearer. If you can get 0.5 mg tablets, use those.

Westward Protocol: Shifting Later

How much to shift, and how fast

Delaying is easier for most people. You can often manage 60–90 minutes of delay per day without much friction. If you have three days before a westward flight crossing eight hours, you can realistically shift 3–4 hours, covering much of the difference.

Example: Normal schedule 11 p.m.–7 a.m., flying eight hours west. Three days out, bed at midnight, alarm at 8 a.m. Two days out: 1:15 a.m. to 9:15 a.m. One day out: 2:30 a.m. to 10:30 a.m. You arrive having shifted 3.5 hours.

Evening light is your main lever

For westward shifts, the goal is to delay the clock. Bright light in the evening does exactly that. Spend time in well-lit spaces after dinner, take a walk outside while it's still light, or sit under bright indoor lighting. Avoid getting blasted with light early in the morning — use blackout curtains, sleep with an eye mask, and don't immediately seek sunlight after waking on pre-shift mornings.

Melatonin for westward shifts

For westward travel, melatonin is less central to the pre-shift strategy. Some people take a small dose on the plane or on the first night at destination to help initiate sleep at an unfamiliar time, but during the pre-shift phase, light management is the primary tool.

Practical Logistics That Trip People Up

Work and social commitments. Shifting your bedtime to 8:30 p.m. is socially disruptive. Be realistic: even a partial shift — one day of aggressive pre-shifting rather than three — is better than nothing. You don't need perfection.

Meal timing. Your digestive clock is also shifted by mealtimes, independently of light. Try to move meals in the same direction as your sleep shift. Eating breakfast earlier when you're advancing, later when you're delaying, adds a secondary cue that reinforces the light signal.

Alcohol and caffeine. Both disrupt sleep architecture and can fragment the early nights of a pre-shift when sleep pressure may already be low because you're going to bed earlier than your body wants. Use caffeine only in the morning during an advance protocol; cut it earlier in the day than usual.

Napping on pre-shift days. Resist the urge to nap if you've gone to bed earlier and woken up tired. Napping bleeds off the sleep pressure that would help you fall asleep at your new earlier target tonight.

On the plane itself. Set your watch to destination time at departure. Sleep and eat as close to destination schedule as possible. Use an eye mask and earplugs to control light and noise exposure. For long eastward flights, a short-acting sleep aid can help you sleep at what your body still perceives as "afternoon" — talk to your doctor if that's relevant.

Common Mistakes

Starting too late. If you begin pre-shifting the night before departure, the disruption to that night's sleep may leave you worse off. Three to five days is the useful window.

Shifting sleep but not light. Sleep timing alone, without corresponding changes to light exposure, will produce only weak clock shifts. Light is not optional — it's the mechanism.

Using high-dose melatonin as a sedative. Melatonin at 5–10 mg is often enough to produce drowsiness, which is why people use it, but the phase-shifting effect is determined by when you take it, not by how much you take. High doses without correct timing may just make you groggy at the wrong time.

Ignoring the return trip. If your trip is short — under five days — it may actually be better not to fully adapt at destination, because you'll be readjusting for the return almost immediately. In that case, a partial pre-shift and some bright-light strategy at destination is often wiser than trying to fully convert.

How Long Does It Take to Fully Recover After Arrival?

Even with a good pre-shift, you'll likely still have some residual jet lag. As a rough guide, expect one day of adjustment for every remaining hour of time difference. Pre-shifting doesn't eliminate jet lag — it shortens and softens it significantly.

Continue the same tools after landing: morning light for eastward destinations, evening light for westward, consistent meal times, and avoiding large daytime naps on the first day or two.

FAQ

How far in advance should I start pre-shifting? Three to five days is the practical window. More than a week out is generally impractical for most people's schedules and the shift gains diminish. Less than two days produces only a small but still useful shift.

Does it work for short trips, like three or four days? For very short trips, partial pre-shifting combined with a deliberate light strategy can be more useful than full adaptation, since you'll be reversing the shift quickly on return.

What if I can't shift my sleep that much without losing too much sleep? A partial shift is better than none. Even one hour of advance or delay in the right direction, combined with proper light management on arrival day, meaningfully reduces peak jet lag symptoms.

Is melatonin safe to use this way? Low-dose melatonin (0.5–1 mg) as a timing cue is considered safe for short-term use by most adults. It is not recommended for children without medical guidance. If you're on any prescription medications, check interactions — melatonin can interact with anticoagulants and some other drugs.

Does the pre-shift strategy work differently for older adults? Older adults tend to have more rigid circadian rhythms and may find phase-advancing especially difficult. The same principles apply, but smaller daily shifts (30 minutes rather than 60) and consistent light exposure become even more important.
