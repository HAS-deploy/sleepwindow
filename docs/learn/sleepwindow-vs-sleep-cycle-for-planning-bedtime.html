SleepWindow vs Sleep Cycle: for planning bedtime

SleepWindow vs Sleep Cycle: Which App Actually Helps You Plan Bedtime?

Both apps promise to fix your mornings by working backward from when your alarm goes off. But they solve the problem differently — and one of them may suit your life far better than the other.

What Each App Is Actually Doing

SleepWindow is a bedtime calculator. You tell it when you need to wake up, and it shows you a set of optimal bedtime windows based on 90-minute sleep cycle intervals. The logic: sleep cycles run roughly 90 minutes each, and waking mid-cycle leaves you groggy. Wake at the end of a cycle and you feel relatively alert. SleepWindow removes the mental math and gives you a clear window — go to bed at this time if you want to complete five full cycles, or this time for four.

Sleep Cycle is a sleep tracker first, bedtime planner second. It uses your phone's microphone or accelerometer to detect movement and sound during the night, then builds up a picture of your actual sleep quality over time. It also has a smart alarm that wakes you during a lighter sleep phase within a 30-minute window before your set alarm.

These are meaningfully different products. One is proactive (plan when to sleep), one is largely reactive (analyze how you slept).

Planning Bedtime: Where SleepWindow Has the Edge

This is the specific job both apps are being asked to do here, and SleepWindow is more direct about it.

Clarity of output. SleepWindow tells you, simply, "go to bed at 10:30 PM, 12:00 AM, or 1:30 AM to wake feeling rested at 7:00 AM." You see all your options at once. There is no setup required, no historical data needed, no account to create. Someone planning their bedtime on a Tuesday afternoon gets a useful answer in seconds.

No hardware dependency. SleepWindow works without leaving your phone on your bed or mattress overnight. If you share a bed, charge your phone across the room, or simply don't want an active microphone near you while you sleep, SleepWindow doesn't ask anything of you.

Useful on night one. Sleep Cycle is genuinely more useful after weeks of data accumulation. SleepWindow is useful immediately, every time.

Predictability. The 90-minute cycle model is a well-established rough guide from sleep science — not infallible, but consistent. SleepWindow applies it cleanly. You always know what logic is driving the recommendation.

Where Sleep Cycle Is Better

It would be misleading to frame this as a clean win for SleepWindow. Sleep Cycle does several things SleepWindow simply cannot.

Actual sleep data. Sleep Cycle records snoring, restlessness, and approximate sleep stages — and graphs them over time. If you want to know whether your sleep quality dropped after a stressful week, whether wine is affecting your deep sleep, or how long you're actually asleep versus in bed, Sleep Cycle gives you that. SleepWindow has no idea what happens after you close your eyes.

The smart alarm. This is Sleep Cycle's flagship feature. Rather than waking you at a fixed time mid-cycle, it listens for lighter sleep within a 30-minute window and triggers the alarm then. For people who find rigid alarms brutal, this makes a meaningful difference in the morning — and real users consistently report it working. SleepWindow cannot do this; it assumes you will wake at the time you set, in whatever sleep state you happen to be in.

Long-term patterns. Sleep Cycle builds a sleep history. Over months, you can spot trends — late nights, seasonal changes, the impact of exercise or alcohol on sleep quality. SleepWindow is stateless. It does not remember anything.

Integrations. Sleep Cycle connects to Apple Health and similar platforms. If you're building a health picture across multiple apps, Sleep Cycle feeds into that ecosystem. SleepWindow does not.

The Underlying Science: Is the 90-Minute Model Reliable?

SleepWindow is built on one assumption: sleep cycles are approximately 90 minutes. This is a reasonable average drawn from sleep research, but it is an average. Individual cycle lengths vary — some people run closer to 80 minutes, others 100. Cycles also shift through the night; the first cycle is often shorter and includes more deep sleep, while later cycles carry more REM.

SleepWindow applies a flat model. Sleep Cycle, theoretically, adapts to your actual patterns over time. In practice, phone-based sleep tracking (especially microphone-based) is less accurate than a clinical sleep study — but it is responding to you, not to a population average.

Neither approach is perfect. SleepWindow's model is transparent and consistent. Sleep Cycle's is personalized but harder to verify.

Pricing

Sleep Cycle has a free tier with limited features, and a paid subscription (approximately $30–40/year, though pricing changes) that unlocks full statistics, trends, and premium features. The smart alarm works on the free version.

SleepWindow is a simpler tool and tends to be free or very low cost. Because it is a calculator rather than a tracking platform, there's no ongoing data infrastructure driving a subscription.

For someone who only wants bedtime planning, SleepWindow is the lower-cost option. For someone who wants the full sleep health picture, Sleep Cycle's subscription may be worth it.

Real Use Cases

You work shifts and your wake time changes constantly. SleepWindow handles this well — recalculate every day in seconds. Sleep Cycle's smart alarm still works, but the historical data becomes harder to interpret when your schedule is irregular.

You share a bed and your partner is a light sleeper. SleepWindow wins. Sleep Cycle's microphone can pick up a partner's movements and sounds, and leaving a phone active on the mattress annoys some people.

**You want to understand why you're tired.** Sleep Cycle wins clearly. SleepWindow offers no insight into sleep quality, disturbances, or patterns.

You're traveling and want a quick bedtime target in a new time zone. SleepWindow is faster and requires no setup.

You want a smarter alarm that wakes you gently. Sleep Cycle wins. SleepWindow cannot adjust when it wakes you.

Pick This If You...

Choose SleepWindow if you want a fast, no-fuss bedtime calculator you can use any night without setup, tracking, or leaving your phone in bed. It's ideal for people who understand the 90-minute cycle logic and simply want the math done for them. It works immediately, every time, with no subscription and no data collection.

Choose Sleep Cycle if you want more than bedtime planning — specifically, a smart alarm that adapts to your sleep phase, ongoing sleep quality data, and insight into patterns over time. You'll need to commit to keeping your phone nearby while you sleep, but if you're willing to do that, Sleep Cycle gives you a genuinely richer picture of your sleep health.

The honest answer: if your only goal is planning what time to go to bed, SleepWindow does that job with less friction. If you want to understand your sleep — not just schedule it — Sleep Cycle is the more complete tool.
